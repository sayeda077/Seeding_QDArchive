// Description of dataset: 
This dataset is from a quasi-experimental study (SOENNING 2011, 2013) that evaluated two methods for teaching phonemic transcription to university students of English: (i) the transcription of auditory stimuli and (ii) an activity-based approach employing a phonemic version of the classic board game Scrabble®. Participants were recruited from three (parallel) "English Phonetics & Phonology" courses, where the final exam involves phonemic transcriptions. A pretest-posttest control group design was used, with group sizes of 9 (control group), 15 (audio group) and 12 (scrabble group). Participants self-selected into two different time slots, which were then assigned to different methods of instruction. The training groups were offered five 90-minute sessions, which were identical for both groups in the first 45 minutes each week, but used different methods in the second half of each session. Transcription ability was assessed prior to and after training using the same (30-minute) test, which covered a wide range of segmental and suprasegmental features, with a maximum of 345 points attainable. The dataset also includes further information on the students, which was elicited using a questionnaire (e.g. whether (and how long) they had spent time abroad, whether they did their homework for the course regularly).

METHODOLOGICAL INFORMATION
--------------------------

// Research design
A pretest-posttest control group design was used for data collection. The dependent variable 'increase in transcription ability' was measured by assessing the level of transcription ability prior to and after treatment. The independent variable, 'teaching method', was experimentally manipulated and had three levels. While a control group received no training, two training groups received training using different methods of instruction. Different participants were used for the experimental conditions.

// Participants
Participants were undergraduate students (studying English at the University of Bamberg) enrolled in an 'English Phonetics & Phonology' course (there were three parallel courses, here labeled as X/Y/Z, taught by the same lecturer, and 84 potential participants). The training was offered as an optional tutorial in the first half of the term and a majority of the students were interested in participating. The pretest was conducted during class and 69 students were recorded. For practical reasons, the allocation of participants to the training groups was not randomized. Students who were not interested in participating in the training automatically formed the control group, which comprised 14 students. Given the unexpectedly high number of volunteers and the fact that the size of the training groups should not exceed 20 participants, a third training group was formed, which is not included in the data for this study. A number of participants dropped out during the course of the study. Participants who attended fewer than four (out of five) training sessions were excluded from the analysis. However, those participants who had attended only one training session (out of five) were assigned to the control group. Several students failed to show up for the posttest. This resulted in group sizes of 9 (control group), 15 (audio group) and 12 (scrabble group) participants. All participants indicated their consent to the data from the pre- and posttest and questionnaire being used for research purposes.

// Test
In order to measure "transcription ability" prior to and after the treatment, a test was designed (see PDF file "transcription_test.pdf"), which consists of two parts: 

* Part 1: "Transcription - Single words". This part contains 60 single words for transcription. Five undemanding items serve as an ice-breaker at the beginning of the list. 
* Part 2: "Transcription - Sentences". This part contains 8 sentences for transcription.

==== Administration =====
The same test was administered as a pretest and posttest (see comments on issues with (and reasons for) this decision further below). In both tests, students were given 20 minutes to complete Part 1. For Part 1, two versions with varying item order were used in the pretest and posttest. After the test sheets for Part 1 had been collected, Part 2 was handed out. Ten minutes were given for this part of the test. 

==== Scoring ====
A total of 345 points were attainable. The total score for Part 1 (60 single words) is 260 points. The number of points attainable for a given word was determined as the sum of six sub-variables (details are given below; see PDF file "transcription_test_scoring.pdf"). The PDF file "transcription_test_design.pdf" gives an overview of these sub-variables and the items used to measure them. Thus, the items in Part 1 cover the complete phoneme inventory and serve to target certain common (German learner) pronunciation and transcription errors (e.g. final devoicing, hypercorrection, neutralized high vowels and various difficult vowel and consonant phonemes). Standard accuracy was assessed with items that have different pronunciations in Standard Southern British English (SSBE) and General American (GA). 

The scoring for the 60 items proceeded as follows:

* VOWELS (86 points in total): One point for every correctly transcribed vowel. The number of vowel points therefore usually corresponds to the number of syllables in a word. Unstressed schwa-vowels with orthography <-er -re> (e.g. "feature", "leather"), however, were excluded from this category since they are covered by the sub-variable STANDARD ACCURACY (see below). Scoring examples (VOWELS only): "put" [1 point], "sixty" [2 points], "toaster" [1 point]

* CONSONANTS (43 points in total): One point for each correctly transcribed consonant phoneme that differs from the orthographic representation. This means that no points were given for the following consonant phonemes: / p b t d k g s m n h r l /. Exceptions: (i) Points were given for the correct transcription of /v/ (despite the orthographic correspondence) since in German Learner English a typical mispronunciation of this phoneme is [w]; (ii) No points were given for word-final /z/, since these cases are covered by the sub-variable FINAL DEVOICING. Scoring examples (CONSONANTS only): "last" [0 points], "wealth" [1 point], "choose" [1 point].

* STANDARD ACCURACY (36 points in total): A point was given if SSBE and GA require different transcriptions and the correct version was transcribed. Students indicated their targeted pronunciation model in the test. Scoring examples (STANDARD ACCURACY only): "home" [1 point], "bottle" [2 points], "potter" [3 points]. 

* FINAL DEVOICING (12 points in total): A point was given for a correctly transcribed final voiced obstruent, but only if the phonemic symbol did not correspond to the orthographic representation (e.g. "serves", "charge", "those"). This means that no point was given for "liquid". 

* STRESS (23 points in total): In words with two or more syllables, a point was given for the correct placement of the stress marker.

* CORRECT TRANSCRIPTIONS (60 points in total): Finally, a point was given for each word that was transcribed without any errors at all.

Neutralized vowels /i u/ were not scored in the pretest since a substantial proportion of students did not distinguish the symbols used for high vowels. The scoring of STANDARD ACCURACY was based on the target variety (SSBE or GA) that participants had indicated. If a participant had not settled on a standard in the pretest, the scoring followed the accent chosen in the posttest.

The total score for Part 2 is 85 points. The scoring for the content words in this part of the test ("bad", "news", "boys", "inside", "known", "where", "stare", "war", "nerves", "what", "mean", "who", "swearing", "talked", "hour") proceeded analogously to that in Part 1, with points assigned for the sub-variables VOWELS, CONCONANTS, STANDARD ACCURACY, FINAL DEVOICING, and STRESS. In addition, Part 2 served to measure aspects of connected speech. Points were given for correct obscuration of essential weak form words, which are marked by brackets in the following listing:

1. I have [some] bad news.
2. [The] boys [are] inside.
3. They should [have] known where it is.
4. Don't stare [at] her.
5. It [was] [a] war [of] nerves.
6. What [does] 'some' mean?
7. Who [were] [you] swearing at?
8. We talked [to] them [for] [an] hour.

For a number of function words in the sentences both the strong and weak form were possible – both were accepted if the strong form was marked for stress and the weak form not. That is, points were given for matching of vowel quality and stress in syllables that could be either strong or weak. These cases are marked by brackets in the following listing:

1. I [have] some bad news.
2. The boys are inside.
3. They [should] have known where it is.
4. Don't stare at [her].
5. It was a war of nerves.
6. What does 'some' mean?
7. Who were you swearing at?
8. We talked to [them] for an hour.
 

==== Issues ====
The same test was used for the pretest and posttest. A number of factors affect the reliability of this method of testing. Memory and practice yield carry-over effects, influencing the second measurement. Another aspect pointed out by ALLEN & YEN is that “[u]ncooperative examinees might object to the second testing and deliberately mismark the second test” (1979: 77). Hence, our initial scheme was to use parallel tests with corresponding items of equal difficulty. A trial of the item pairs revealed a high unreliability of this method since especially students with a lower level of transcription ability made unsystematic mistakes. When conducting the posttest on June 10th and 11th, the author announced that a copy of this test would be corrected and returned. This effort ensured a higher degree of cooperation since it can be assumed that most students would appreciate feedback on their transcription ability. 


// Teaching methods
The focus of the study is to investigate the efficiency of two classroom activities for teaching phonemic transcription:

AUDITORY METHOD. The basic exercise format employed in the audio-based method is the transcription of recorded stimuli, which include SSBE and GA variants (further details are provided below, in the section "Experimental manipulation: Audio group"). The goal of the auditory method is twofold. It focuses on the command of the phoneme inventory as well as on the rules governing pronunciation and transcription conventions, i.e. aspects of transcription that are 'learnable'. Secondly, the avoidance of orthographic stimuli not just reduces mistakes due to a falsely internalized pronunciation but aims to establish a psychological distance between written words and their pronunciation, which is a necessary step for the acquisition of transcription skills.

PHONEME SCRABBLE. The second method takes an activity-based approach to teaching transcription. Despite the fact that the use of games in education is an acclaimed method, their employment at university level is rather unusual. For the present study a phonemic adaption of the classic board game Scrabble® was designed, including an SSBE and a GA version. Players draw phoneme tiles, build words in crossword fashion on a game board and receive points for completed words. Points are based on the value of the symbols. Frequent phonemes are not as valuable as rare or difficult ones, such as neutralized vowels and the consonants /ʒ ð/. The points assigned to the symbols are not only based on the frequency of their occurrence (taken from CRYSTAL 2003: 239, 242), but also on pedagogical priorities. Thus, non-phonemic symbols, diphthongs, and difficult vowels and consonants receive higher values. An overview of the tiles can be found in the PDF file "phoneme_scrabble_tiles.pdf". The pedagogical potential of this game stems from the fact that it reverses the typical processes involved in transcription. It does not present a stimulus which is then transferred into phonemes. Contrary to this passive role, the learner is presented with a number of symbols and must actively create a word from the sounds represented by these symbols. The immediate objective of phoneme scrabble is to improve the command of the phoneme inventory. A crucial aspect of playing phoneme scrabble is the use of a pronunciation dictionary. While trying to build words from the drawn set of symbols, students consult a dictionary to check if the sound sequences they have composed represent the intended word. The players actively explore the target-language sound structure and autonomously correct their mistakes. Apart from the creative and entertaining potential of building words from phonemes, the communicative aspect of this board game stems from the discussions arising from sound chains laid out on the game board. Phoneme scrabble also allows for targeting certain aspects of transcription. Thus, through the modification of the number and types of tiles, certain aspects of transcription and pronunciation can be emphasized. For example, the number of neutralized vowels can be increased if a lesson aims at the active application of their distribution rules.

// Experimental manipulation: Shared part
Consisting of five weekly sessions of 90 minutes, the trainings took place in the same room and at the same time (8 to 9.30 pm) on Mondays (audio group) and Tuesdays (scrabble group). The treatment in the first 45 minutes of each session was identical for both groups. All materials used in the shared part are made available in an OSF project (SOENNING 2022), in the following folders:

* "Training - Materials" > "PowerPoint slides (both training groups)"). 
* "Training - Materials" > "Handouts (both training groups)" 

The first 45 minutes followed a similar pattern every week with each session focusing on a different aspect of transcription. At the beginning (materials: "PowerPoint slides (both training groups)"), a cartoon or picture was presented to discuss pronunciation difficulties that German learners of English might experience. Next, a text in phonemic transcription was projected. Students took turns reading out loud sentence by sentence. This 'warm-up' aimed at making participants more familiar with transcription symbols. Where necessary, the instructor corrected students' pronunciations, paying particular attention to standard accuracy. The following 30 minutes were used to work on practice sheets emphasizing a certain aspect of transcription (see "Handouts (both training groups)"). Participants were asked to complete the exercises and a discussion of the results followed. 

* The first session focused on vowels and included the transcription of monophthongs and diphthongs. With the majority of students being unfamiliar with non-phonemic high vowels, special attention was paid to the neutralized /i/. Rules for its distribution were listed on the sheet.

* Session two dealt with difficult consonants. Here, exercises differed for SSBE and GA, and students chose the exercise matching their targeted standard accent. Students conforming to the British standard were presented a passage from the Bible in which they had to identify all the linking-r sounds. The GA version of this task included an excerpt from the Constitution of the United States, in which all words containing a flap-t had to be marked.

* The third session emphasized stress in single words. The main aim was to practice the reduction of vowels in unstressed syllables and stress shift resulting from suffixation. The worksheet also introduced the neutralized /u/, giving the rules of its distribution and an exercise. The introduction of the non-phonemic vowels was essential for Session four.

* Session four concentrated on weak forms of function words. While students were reading the projected phonemic text, the instructor paid special attention to a correct pronunciation of obscured vowels. An explanation of the nature and occurrence of weak forms and their strong counterparts (e.g. "for": weak form in "It's for me."; strong form in "Who is it for?") was given before the worksheets were handed out. In an excerpt from Alice in Wonderland, italicized weak form words had to be transcribed. The students worked in groups on different paragraphs. The solutions were presented on the overhead projector and discussed afterwards.

* The last session focused on syntactic stress. Task one consisted of seven sentences in which the stressed syllables had to be marked. These sentences raised several issues, including stress in compounds compared to free noun phrases, e.g. /ˈɪŋglɪʃ tiːʧə/ vs. /ˈɪŋglɪʃ ˈtiːʧə/, word classes distinguished by stress, e.g. /ˈrekɔːd/ vs. /rɪˈkɔːd/, and compounds with different stress patterns in SSBE and GA, e.g. /hedˈmɑːstə/ (SSBE) vs. /ˈhedmæstɚ/ (GA). The second exercise included ten short sentences for transcription. A number of words (were, do, be, for) occurred in two different contexts requiring either the strong or the weak form. In order to exemplify the effect of the phonetic environment on the final vowel of a weak form, five words occurred in both prevocalic and preconsonatic position (do, the, to, you, be). Further, cases were given in which the strong form of weak form words was essential, e.g. "were" in citation form and "be"/"for" in final position. To minimize the variation in solutions, one stress pattern was agreed on for each sentence before students started transcribing. 

This mainly theoretical training part served as a basis for the second half. In this practical part, the two training groups experienced different treatment, which consisted of activities aiming at reinforcing the newly acquired knowledge.

// Experimental manipulation: Audio group
With the transcription of auditory stimuli being the central element of this method, the audio group received worksheet-based exercises focusing on the main topic of the training session (the classroom materials are available at https://osf.io/vxgmu/, in the folder "Training - Materials" > "Handouts - Audio group"). Participants transcribed words or sentences that were played on the classroom sound system. For the transcription of single words in the first three sessions, items were chosen in accordance with the emphasized aspect of transcription. Recordings included in the CD-ROM version of the Cambridge English Pronouncing Dictionary (Jones 2006) served as audio stimuli. The folded worksheets covered the orthographic versions of the items. When the task was completed, students unfolded the paper and compared their solutions. All exercises concentrated on receptive ability except for one minimal pair activity in session two. Split into two groups, participants received a worksheet listing 31 sentences. The sentences of group A differed in one phoneme from those of group B. Students took turns pronouncing these sentences and tried to identify the minimal pairs. The subject of session four was the transcription of weak form words in connected speech. The exercises included sentences with gaps leaving out function words. Upon hearing the recorded pronunciation students had to decide whether the missing words were pronounced in their weak or strong form and transcribe them. The exercises in the last session which involved the transcription of complete sentences.

// Experimental manipulation: Scrabble group
The scrabble group spent the second half of the training playing phoneme scrabble in groups of two or three players. These groups had to be uniformly SSBE or GA and each table needed to have at least one pronunciation dictionary for reference. In the first session the rules were introduced, then the participants played. Each week the set of tiles was modified to increase the occurrence of symbols introduced or discussed in the theoretical part of the training. The role of the instructor was to consult students and, more importantly, correct mistakes on the game board. These often involved a violation of what has been referred to as 'transcription rules'. The instructor corrected false transcriptions and explained the source of the error.
